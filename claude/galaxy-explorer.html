The Log Truth

What this map is hiding from you

Galaxy sizes: inflated for visibility
Galaxies here are drawn roughly 5–10× their true angular size relative to the distances between them. At true scale, they'd be tiny specks separated by vast, vast emptiness. The Local Group would look like three dim dots in a sea of nothing.
Distances: compressed but proportional
The relative positions use real astronomical data (RA, Dec, redshift-independent distances). Andromeda really is ~2.5 Mly away and the Virgo Cluster really is ~54 Mly. But the 3D projection compresses the depth to fit your screen.
What you can't see: the emptiness between
If the Milky Way were a dinner plate, Andromeda would be another plate about 20 feet away. The nearest large galaxy cluster (Virgo) would be 400 feet away. This map makes it look like a busy neighborhood. It's actually a desert.
Each dot contains billions of stars
Every galaxy sprite on this map represents 10 million to 1 trillion individual stars, each surrounded by its own planets and moons. The Milky Way alone has more stars than every grain of sand on every beach on Earth.
Zoom in on one? That's the Milky Way map.
Click the Milky Way link to explore inside a single galaxy. Then click Solar System to explore inside a single star system. Three maps, three levels of logarithmic lies, all necessary to make the invisible visible.
